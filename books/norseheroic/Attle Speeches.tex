\section{Introduction}

The \textbf{Greenlandish Speeches of Attle} (\textlink{Atlamal}) is only preserved in \Regius\ where it follows \textlink{Atlakvida} and precedes \textlink{Gudrunarhvot}.  The poem is composed in \Malahattr\ throughout.

In \Regius\ the title Greenlandish is also applied to the preceding \textlink{Atlakvida}, but unlike that poem \Atlamal\ appears actually to have been composed in one of the Norse settlements on Greenland, for in st. 18 the poet makes reference to a “white-bear” (\emph{hvíta-bjǫrn}).  The polar bear (\emph{Ursus maritimus}) is indeed found on Greenland, but not on Iceland or the Scandinavian peninsula—to what inhospitable northern wastes the Norse had brought these old tales about Attila the Hun!

A Greenlandish origin for the poem lets us to determine a \emph{terminus post quem} for its composition; \Atlamal\ cannot have been composed before ca. 985 CE, the time of Greenland’s settlement by Icelanders agreed upon both by the mediæval saws and modern archeology.
This fits with the fact that the poem from a linguistic perspective is noticably younger than its predecessor \textlink{Atlakvida}.  Most notably the sound change \emph{vr-} > \emph{r-} is consistently applied in roots originally containing the glide.  These are \emph{vręiðr} > \emph{ręiðr} ‘wroth’ (\textlink{Atlamal}[51]/1: \emph{rǫskr, rǿða} : \emph{ręiðr}, 75/4: \emph{ró, ręiði} : \emph{ręynir} vs. \textlink{Atlakvida}[2]/2: \emph{vïn, val-hǫllu} : \emph{vręiði}),
\emph{vrangr} > \emph{rangr} ‘wrong, twisted’ (\textlink{Atlamal}[4]/1: \emph{ru̇nar, rísta} : \emph{ręngði} vs. Bragi \emph{Þórr} 6 (in \Skp\ 3): \emph{vildi-t, vrǫngum : vágs}),
and \emph{*vrǫskr} > \emph{rǫskr} ‘vigorous’ (\textlink{Atlamal}[51]/1, 57/2: \emph{rǫskr, ręynas’k : ręynt}, 87/1: \emph{rǫskr, rǿða : rakðis’k} vs. \Thorsdrapa\ 22/1: \emph{vręiðr, Vrǫsku : vá}).  For the root \emph{vreka} > \emph{reka} ‘drive, chase’ which is not found in \Atlamal\ cf. \textlink{Atlakvida}[14]/4.

There are other important differences between the two poems that speak to the primacy of \textlink{Atlakvida}.  One of the most interesting differences is the \Atlamal-poet’s lack of familiarity with continental European circumstances.  Whereas the poet of \textlink{Atlakvida} has Guther and Hain ride through Mirkwood and over high mountains to get to Attle in the south (\textlink{Atlakvida}[14]), \Atlamal\ instead lets Guther and Hain \emph{sail} out of their home firth (\emph{fjǫrðr}) to the land of Budle, in what is clearly an insular Norse adaptation of the originally continental story (\textlink{Atlamal}[28]–35).

Together with \textlink{Atlakvida}, \Atlamal\ clearly serves as a source for \VolsungaSaga\ 36–38.  For much of that section \Atlamal\ is the chief source, probably due to its greater length.
